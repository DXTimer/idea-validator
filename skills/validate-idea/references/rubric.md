# Scoring rubric & verdict bands

Score each dimension **1–5** (sum out of 50). Each score gets a one-line
justification grounded in a specific panel finding — never a bare number.

The score informs the verdict; it does not *decide* it. Read the **capping rules**
below: idea quality is not an average. One structural zero sinks the whole thing.

## Dimensions (1–5 each, max 50)

| # | Dimension | 1 (fatal) | 3 (workable) | 5 (strong) |
|---|-----------|-----------|--------------|------------|
| 1 | **Problem severity** | Nice-to-have nobody asks for | Real but tolerated | Hair-on-fire painkiller |
| 2 | **Customer pull** | "Cool, I guess" | Would try if free | Asking for it / hacking their own version |
| 3 | **Market size & timing** | Tiny or shrinking | Decent, flat | Big and growing, clear why-now |
| 4 | **Willingness to pay** | No budget exists | Would pay a little | Clear budget, already paying for worse |
| 5 | **Differentiation / moat** | Trivially copyable | Some lead time | Real defensibility (data/network/distribution) |
| 6 | **Distribution / reachability** | No way to reach buyers | A channel exists, expensive | Cheap, obvious channel to first 100 |
| 7 | **Feasibility / unit economics** | Miracle required or CAC>LTV | Hard but doable | Clearly buildable, economics gesture at working |
| 8 | **Founder-market fit** *(score 3 if unknown)** | Wrong person entirely | Could learn it | Unusually well-suited / unfair edge |
| 9 | **Defensible why-now** | No real shift | Mild tailwind | Strong, recent structural shift |
| 10 | **Risk testability** | Riskiest bet can't be cheaply tested | Testable with effort | One cheap experiment de-risks it |

> Dimension 8: if founder-market fit is unknown, score it 3 (neutral) and say so —
> don't penalize or reward what you can't see.

## Capping rules (a fatal flaw overrides the sum)

Apply these **after** summing. State which rule fired (or "none").

- **Market of nobody.** Dim 3 = 1 → verdict caps at **PASS or PARK**, regardless of
  total.
- **No moat + incumbent threat.** Dim 5 = 1 AND the Competitor agent found an
  incumbent who can ship it as a feature → caps at **PURSUE-REFRAMED at best**
  (the wedge must change).
- **No painkiller + no pull.** Dim 1 ≤ 2 AND Dim 2 ≤ 2 → caps at **PARK** until the
  problem is reframed to something acute.
- **Untestable leap.** Dim 10 = 1 → caps at **PARK**. You cannot recommend PURSUE on
  a bet you can't cheaply check.
- **No path to customers.** Dim 6 = 1 → caps at **PARK or PASS**. A great product
  nobody can be sold is not a business.

If no cap fires, map the raw total to a band as a *starting point*, then adjust with
judgment:

| Total | Starting band |
|-------|---------------|
| 40–50 | PURSUE (high confidence) |
| 32–39 | PURSUE or PURSUE-REFRAMED |
| 24–31 | PARK or PURSUE-REFRAMED — the core may be real, the framing isn't |
| < 24  | PASS, unless one dimension is a 5 worth reframing around |

## The four verdicts

- **PURSUE** — riskiest assumption is cheaply testable, upside justifies it. Next
  step is the test, not the build.
- **PURSUE, REFRAMED** — the core is real but customer/wedge/framing is wrong. State
  the specific pivot in one sentence.
- **PARK** — not now. Name the one condition that would flip it to PURSUE.
- **PASS** — structural fatal flaw, no cheap test changes it. Say why plainly.

## Confidence (separate from verdict)

- **High** — research-grounded, panel largely agreed, riskiest bet is clear.
- **Medium** — some unverified claims or panel disagreement; verdict could move with
  one data point.
- **Low** — too much unknown; the real output is "go get this specific data first."

Confidence is about *how sure you are of the verdict*, not how good the idea is. A
confident PASS and a confident PURSUE are both valuable. A low-confidence anything
means the next step is data collection.
