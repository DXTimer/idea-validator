# Offer Playbook — business knowledge for the Offer Architect

The Offer Architect ("The Closer") uses this. The goal of an early offer is not to
maximize revenue. It is to make a **yes** so cheap and obvious that a real buyer
commits real money, because a paid commitment is the only validation signal that
doesn't lie. Design the offer so the act of buying *is* the experiment.

## 1. The value equation (decide if the offer is even good)

```
              Dream Outcome  ×  Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
  Value  =  ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                  Time Delay  ×  Effort & Sacrifice
```

Pull every lever:
- **Dream outcome ↑** — sell the result they already want, in their words and numbers
  ("recover one $25k placement"), not the mechanism ("an AI agent").
- **Perceived likelihood ↑** — proof, guarantees, doing it by hand, naming the avatar
  so they think "that's me." A human concierge has near-100% perceived likelihood;
  software has to earn it.
- **Time delay ↓** — deliver a win in hours/days, not months. Front-load found-money
  (e.g. "day 1 I dig out the deals already rotting").
- **Effort & sacrifice ↓** — they should do almost nothing. A 20-minute setup beats a
  "migrate your data" ask every time.

A weak offer is almost always weak on the bottom of the equation (slow, or too much
work for the buyer). Fix that before touching price.

## 2. The Grand Slam Offer (so good they feel stupid saying no)

Stack until the perceived value dwarfs the price:
- **Core deliverable** — the one outcome.
- **Bonuses that each kill a specific objection.** Don't add random extras. List the
  buyer's top 3 reasons to say no, then add a bonus that removes each. ("No time to
  set up" → done-for-you setup. "Won't know if it worked" → results report.)
- **Risk reversal** — see §4. The single biggest lever on conversion.
- **Scarcity** — a real cap ("I can only hand-run 5 at once"). Fake scarcity is
  poison; real scarcity from a real constraint is honest and converts.
- **Urgency** — a real deadline (founding cohort closes, price goes up at launch).
- **A name** — name the offer ("The Saved Placement Pilot") and the avatar. Named
  things feel real and specific; generic things feel skippable.

## 3. Cash-flow mechanics (get paid upfront, on purpose)

Upfront cash from the right buyer funds the work AND proves demand. Prefer these:
- **Pre-sell before you build.** Sell the outcome, deliver the first round by hand
  (concierge / Wizard-of-Oz). Cash before code.
- **Deposit-for-credit.** "Pay $X now, get 2X credit at launch." The buyer feels
  they're getting free money; you get cash, commitment, and a list of real customers
  waiting. (Example: $99 deposit → $199 launch credit. Treat the numbers as
  adjustable, not gospel.)
- **Founding / charter pricing for life.** Early buyers lock a permanent low rate
  (e.g. $49/mo when list will be $99-149). Rewards the believers, creates urgency,
  and the lifetime discount costs little against a customer who'd have churned anyway.
- **Annual prepay.** "2 months free if you pay yearly." Pulls a year of cash forward
  and slashes churn.
- **Pay-only-if-it-works.** For services/pilots: tie payment to a delivered result.
  Maximum perceived likelihood; you only forgo revenue when you'd have failed anyway.

Rule of thumb: a card on file is signal; "I'll pay when it's ready" is not. If they
won't put money down now, you have not validated willingness to pay, you have
validated politeness.

## 4. Risk reversal / guarantees (remove the buyer's downside)

The buyer's fear, not your price, is usually what kills the sale. Reverse it:
- **Unconditional** — full refund, no questions. Simple, high-trust.
- **Conditional / outcome** — "If I don't catch one follow-up worth more than the fee,
  it's free." Ties the guarantee to the dream outcome; filters tire-kickers.
- **Better-than-money-back** — refund AND they keep a bonus. Removes the last hesitation.
- Stronger guarantee → higher conversion, and it forces YOU to make the offer actually
  deliver. If you can't stomach the guarantee, the offer isn't good enough yet.

## 5. The offer IS the experiment (tie back to validation)

The riskiest assumption for most ideas is "will the target buyer pay for this?" An
offer answers it for real:
- Pitch a known number of exact-avatar prospects (e.g. 50 warm).
- State a **kill number** up front: "if fewer than N pay the deposit, the wedge is
  dead." (A common bar: under ~10-15% conversion on warm, qualified outreach is a
  red flag for a paid pre-sale.)
- Watch the **second-order signal** too: of those who pay for the manual/pilot
  version, how many convert to the real product? Low conversion means you built a
  service, not a product.

Money is the only customer-development answer that can't be polite. Interviews tell
you what people believe; a deposit tells you what they'll do.

## 6. Anti-patterns (don't ship these)

- Discounting before adding value. A cheaper bad offer is still a bad offer.
- Fake scarcity / fake deadlines. One discovered lie taints everything.
- Free pilots "to get feedback." Free attracts people who won't pay and gives a false
  green light. Charge something, even small.
- A guarantee you secretly hope no one invokes. If it scares you, the product isn't
  ready, fix that.
- Selling the mechanism ("AI-powered agentic platform") instead of the outcome
  ("never lose a $25k placement to a dropped follow-up").
